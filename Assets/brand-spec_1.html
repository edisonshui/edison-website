07Playground

Things you can
actually use.

The playground is not a second projects page. The distinction is the difference between reading about something and doing it. /projects is formal — descriptions, stack, links, recruiter-readable. /playground is experiential — the work is embedded and alive, and you interact with it directly on the page.

The rule: If you can't interact with it on the page, it doesn't belong here — it belongs on /projects. A chess engine card with a "view on GitHub" link is a project. A chess engine you can play against is playground.

08Video

Two videos.
Two purposes.

The site uses video in exactly two places. Each serves a completely different function. They should never be confused with each other.

Video 01 · Hero
Atmospheric wallpaper
Sets tone. Gets out of the way. Loops invisibly. Nobody reads it — they feel it. Ink diffusion in cream water.
Video 02 · Montage
Earned personality moment
Content, not wallpaper. Placed after Projects — after the visitor has a reason to care about you as a person.

Video 01 — Hero background

Ink already diffused into warm cream water, shot from directly overhead. The ink is mid-settle — barely moving, the physics of diffusion continuing at an almost imperceptible rate. Loops seamlessly because the start and end frame are virtually identical.

Image prompt — generate this first (Midjourney, Firefly, Stable Diffusion)
Overhead top-down view of a wide shallow ceramic dish filled with still, slightly cloudy cream-colored water. Dark ink — warm charcoal and deep umber tones — has already diffused into the water and is mid-settle: organic tendrils and soft cloud-like plumes spread across the right two-thirds of the frame. The left third of the frame is mostly clear, lightly tinted cream water with only the faintest ink wisps at the edges. The ink patterns are delicate and intricate, with soft feathered edges, not hard lines. Lighting is natural, soft, and directional — warm morning light from the upper left casting a very subtle sheen across the water surface. The ceramic dish has a matte off-white rim just barely visible at the edges. No bubbles, no ripples, no movement — perfectly still. Color palette: background water #F5F2EB cream, ink tones #1C1A14 warm black and #5A4A30 umber. Shot with a 50mm macro lens, extremely flat depth of field, medium format film texture, ultra high resolution. No text. No people.
Video prompt — feed image above as first frame (Kling 2.0, Runway Gen-4, or Sora)
Completely static overhead camera, zero camera movement whatsoever — no pan, no tilt, no zoom, no drift, no shake. The scene from the first frame continues with only the physics of the ink and water: the diffused ink patterns continue their extremely slow, almost imperceptible outward migration through the cream-colored water. The movement is unhurried and meditative — the tendrils advance by only a few millimeters over the full duration of the clip. The ink edges soften and feather further as they spread. No new ink is introduced. No drops fall. No wind disturbs the surface. No ripples. The water remains glassy and still except for the invisible physics of diffusion. The light does not change. The final frame looks nearly identical to the first frame. Duration: 8–10 seconds. Designed to loop seamlessly and invisibly. Mood: silent, patient, meditative.
Hero text placement over the video
Text content
Line 1: EDISON — Cormorant 300, ~80px, letter-spacing: 0.06em
Line 2: Building things. Writing thoughts. — Cormorant italic 300, ~20px, color: #8A8070
Position
position: absolute
bottom: 10vh; left: 8vw
Over the clear-water left third. No backdrop or blur behind text. The cream water provides contrast.

Looping: In your video editor apply a 1–2 second crossfade dissolve between the last and first frame. At this motion level, the loop is completely invisible. Alternatively use CSS animation-direction: alternate to reverse the clip — forward then backward — which is even more seamless.

Video 02 — Moments montage

A personal supercut. Real moments, handheld or candid, edited to feel unhurried. This is the section where the site goes from "a developer portfolio" to "a person I want to know." Placed on the homepage between Projects and the Graph teaser — after the visitor has earned the context to appreciate it.

Duration
45–90 seconds. Long enough to feel human, short enough to hold attention without autoplay controls becoming awkward. Do not exceed 2 minutes.
Content
Mix of: building / coding moments, chess, being outside, friends, New York, anything that shows life outside a screen. Specificity beats polish — a shaky clip from a real moment beats a crisp stock-feeling shot.
Editing
No jump cuts. Slow cuts, 2–4 seconds per clip. Let each moment breathe. No text overlays, no captions, no title cards inside the video. The visuals carry it.
Color grade
Warm and slightly desaturated. Pull the highlights toward cream (#F5F2EB) and the shadows toward warm black (#1C1A14). Nothing cool-toned. Consistent across all clips.
Audio
Muted by default with a visible unmute button — never autoplay with sound. If you include music, one track only, something unhurried and instrumental. No copyright issues: use Epidemic Sound, Artlist, or a royalty-free ambient track.
Layout
Full-bleed width, 16:9 aspect ratio. Eyebrow label above: "moments" in DM Sans 500, letter-spacing 0.1em. A single sentence in Cormorant italic below the video — something genuine, not a caption, more like a note.
Caption example
"Some of the things I'm building toward."

Why it's here and not in the hero: A montage in the hero is asking someone to watch content before they know if they care. The ink video sets a tone without demanding attention. The montage, placed after Writing and Projects, is earned — by then the visitor already has a reason to want to know you as a person. The sequence matters.

09Signature feature

The photo
graph

An Obsidian-style interactive force graph — but instead of connecting notes, it connects photos. Your life as a network. Nodes are photos. Edges are shared tags (place, event, date, theme). Built with D3.js force simulation.

Nodes

Photos rendered as circles, clipped round
Node size = number of connections (degree)
Hover: photo expands, tags appear below

Edges

Drawn between photos sharing ≥1 tag
Opacity scales with number of shared tags
Sage #7A8B6F · 0.8px · animated on load

Interactions

Drag nodes freely
Scroll to zoom in/out
Click node → full photo modal + linked nodes highlight
Filter by tag → non-matching nodes fade to 10% opacity

Data schema

{
  id: "photo-042",
  src: "/photos/042.jpg",
  date: "2025-05-12",
  tags: ["chess", "nyc",
        "senior-year"],
  caption: "optional"
}

The entrance transition: Navigating to /graph fades the cream background to #0F0E0B over 800ms. The graph nodes drift in from the center. This moment — the shift from warm light to dark canvas — is the most important single interaction on the site.

11Voice & tone

Voice

Write the way you talk to someone smart who isn't in your CS program. Specific and honest. No jargon without context. No résumé-speak. First person throughout.

don't write this
"I have experience with data structures, algorithms, and full-stack development using modern frameworks and best practices."
write this instead
"I've built a chess engine that beats me about 70% of the time, and websites for three restaurants in my neighborhood."
don't write this
"In this post, I will explore the implementation of alpha-beta pruning as an optimization technique for game tree search algorithms."
write this instead
"I kept wondering why my chess AI would blunder obvious pieces. Turns out it was evaluating 8 million nodes a second and still playing like my little cousin."
Post titles: lowercase. "building a chess engine from scratch" not "Building A Chess Engine From Scratch"
Lead with the interesting thing. Don't bury the lede in "background" or "motivation" sections.
Short paragraphs. Max 3–4 sentences. Blog reading is scanning. Give the eye places to land.
Be specific about failure. "I got this wrong for two days" is more interesting than "after some debugging."
No skills section. "JavaScript, Python, React" on a resume is fine. On a personal site it reads as insecure. Let the projects speak.
